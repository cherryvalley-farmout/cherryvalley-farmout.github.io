Farm Out

The Singing Tree

by Melissa Horvath & Doris Lange

May 7, 1974 — The Freeman’s Journal

❦

Spring has come finally and, as usual, suddenly. One day we have snow and it seems a week later all is green. I think it was Mark Twain who said “If you don’t like the weather in these parts, don’t complain, just wait five minutes and it’ll change.”

Spring arrives officially at our house when the singing tree is in blossom. We have at least twenty maples around the house, but only one of them is the singing tree. The singing tree blossoms earlier than the rest — a fact that the bees for miles around know. It is a wonderfully mystical spring thing to hear a tree sing.

There are many prosaic signs of spring not usually mentioned by writers. Spring has come when you go into the house to cool off — when your car seat is too hot to set on (don’t forget this one) — when you have to clean out closets and drawers to put away winter clothes and get out summer ones — when the kids want to play ball instead of watch tv — when friends bike over for a visit — when we hear the hum of tractors from all directions — when motorcycles zoom by at strange hours — and when dinner is late because you’ve been outside all day and haven’t seen a clock.

One definition of an optimist is a person who plants a tree he will never see mature. We’re planting seedlings at Farm Out for the pleasure of our yet-to-be-born grandchildren.

In the long run we’re optimists. In the short run we expect snow and frost if not in May — in June. But for now we’re enjoying spring and hope all of you are too.
