by Melissa Horvath & Doris Lange

May 21, 1974 — The Freeman’s Journal

We had a lesson in patience this week to share with Farm Out readers:

For Five Full Minutes — Filled with Frustration —

I saw only the rear end of that…soo-o-o-slow —

Gray car; And the back of that old man’s head.

My head kept saying, “Hurry — come on — hurry up!”

And there was no place to pass….

 

While all around me the dandelions sparkled

In the warm spring sunshine;

The varied pastels of the budding trees

Glowed against a so-blue sky;

And the brilliant green of spring grass surrendered

To the rich dark earth beneath the plow….

 

I almost forgot to pass my slow-moving friend

When I got the chance.

Cow-Slips and Broken Fences

Every spring, no matter how carefully we have checked the pasture fence, the cows find that one weak place — or several weak places, one at a time. We don’t mind rounding up stray cattle on a bright spring day; there’s always something new or pleasant to see and to talk about later. Sometimes we see the beavers at work on a new house, and there are so many kinds of birds! Their songs are music to these farmers’ ears.

Our cows break fences, but have you ever seen a cow-slip under a fence? Actually, the cows walk around the wet places where the cow-slips grow, but as long as we’re here, we can gather a bouquet and a great bunch of leaves to use as salad or cooked greens tonight. We arrive home with pockets and a “jacket-turned-into-a-bag,” loaded with cow-slip leaves, cat-tail shoots, and wild onions…still calling, “Come boss, come boss…” It can really be fun — once or twice. But the third or fourth cattle drive on a cold rainy day is sure to convince someone that it is time to really fix the fence.

Get Going — The Force of Nature Is on Your Side

We’re under the impression that lots of people are becoming gardeners this year for the first time — or, if they had always gardened just for fun, this year they are gardening in earnest! If you haven’t started yet, GET GOING! Almost any patch of earth can become a garden with a little tender loving care.

Don’t be frightened off by too much advice: “Test the soil”…“Buy this or that fertilizer”… Some gardening books make the process seem more difficult than the alchemist dream of producing gold from base metals. Our advice: don’t take all that advice too seriously. Seeds that are put in the soil can’t keep from growing! With just a little common sense and elbow grease, you beginners will have both vegetables and fun. Don’t forget, you have a very strong force on your side — the force of nature! We can practically guarantee that the little garden you thought you’d start this spring will look a whole lot larger in the middle of July!
