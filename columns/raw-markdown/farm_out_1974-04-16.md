# The Farmer's Journal — April 16, 1974

## FARM OUT!
*by Melissa Horvath & Doris Lange*

A new expression has found a place in our vocabularies — "Farm Out!" At first it may sound like everyone else, but on the farm it's not surprising that we say "Farm out!" instead. [Everyone wants credit for inventing the phrase, so I'll claim authorship, just to settle the controversy.]

Of course, to "farm out" is a typical work-a-day verb phrase. My dictionary says "to send work from a central point to be done elsewhere." How I wish, sometimes, that it could be done elsewhere! But we hope that FARM OUT will be about fun, good ideas and things we notice — and we'll try to share the joys we find in our work-a-day lives.

We had both definitions in mind when we began to discuss ideas for a column — "Farm Out" being a relatively new way to say "Wow! Exciting or Extraordinary" and also something we do whenever we talk to others — and when we listen!

We have some experiences and ideas to share, so do you! We'd like this to be everyone's column, not just ours.

As near as we can tell, "FARM OUT" will have a lot of Cherry Valley in it, because this is where we both live, but we welcome farm-out comment, large and small from all of you. Cherry Valley has a delightful past and present tradition of extraordinary people — we hope to share with you some of the stories about these folks, but everyday experiences of ordinary people can take on extraordinary aspects when viewed from "FARM OUT."

Melissa's day began with the other day when the restaurant sign in the window at Rosset's Garage read "Today is Old." "Well, Howard," Melissa said to herself, "aren't most days?"

We've been reading about a system of sewage and middle distillation that might help relieve the energy crisis. Several people in various parts of the world are producing a practical alternative fuel from the methane gas produced by the "breaking down" of sewage. Tommy says that might be one practical way to clean up Cherry Valley Creek too! Does anyone have ideas about non-conventional energy? Please send your information to this column.

Tommy says he sees a first faint flush of green in the pasture, and he knows it isn't green grass — that green is not the new green of spring, but the old green that survived the winter. Perhaps it's because he's nearsighted — I'd believe he was right and didn't argue.

Janey told us that a log splits better if you split the air with the maul when you tap with the wedge — and she knew that before I asked her to chop up the old apple tree for firewood. Such a waste!

Why on earth don't more people harvest their own maple syrup? I feel so sorry for you folks with beautiful old maple trees around your yards, and no syrup being made. Such a waste!

## SUGARIN' TIME

They say that wood is the only fuel that warms you twice: once when you cut it, and again when you burn it. Maybe syrup-making envelops you twice also — first in the making and all year long (I hope) on the pancakes!

How great it is to know — occasionally — An Extraordinary day!

Bright sky, turning brighter at day's end;
Maple trees, sweetening to syrup;
Chill of the wind, and warmth of the fire.
The smell of the sap in the clean spring air;
Chrystaline call of the Reedbirds;
Harmonizing with friend Kilee-e-e-e-e-r
Of the red-tails to fill my ears;
To over flowing!

Today I will not feel the pain — the tension — the fatigue — the boredom
Or the mindless routine of ordinary days!
